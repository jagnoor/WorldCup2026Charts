Executive verdict

Across eight matches, ten major contested officiating decisions were identified from match data, referee analysts, and formal protests. Eight of the ten broke in Argentina's favor. Impact-weighted (red or goal-value = 3, penalty = 2, yellow = 1), the ledger reads +17 for Argentina against 4 the other way — a net of +13. That is the strongest evidence in this report, and it is qualitative: a one-directional pattern in the calls people argued about.

The counted numbers tell a flatter story than the narrative did. With exact primary-feed counts now in every cell, Argentina committed 113 fouls and took 13 yellows plus one red across the tournament — one booking per 8.7 fouls, marginally stricter than the ~9.5-foul field average, and near-identical to their own opponents' 8.9. The mid-tournament "one card per 19.7 fouls" figure that anchored the leniency storyline came from flawed secondary reporting (the same reporting that inverted the Jordan foul counts) and is corrected here. No discipline-count test on primary data clears — or even approaches — significance.

What remains is what was always strongest: the direction test on contested calls lands between p ≈ 0.03 and p ≈ 0.15 depending on which incidents you count; two of the three heaviest pro-Argentina decisions (the Zico disallowed goal, the Embolo red card) were judged protocol-correct by officiating experts even as opposing federations protested; and the confounders are real — controversies involving the most-watched team get catalogued while identical calls elsewhere do not. The strongest counter-evidence is the ending itself: Argentina lost the final 1–0 after a red card that referee analysts called correct, while Spain played 21 fouls without a single booking.

Overall verdict — unchanged, now on firmer data A consistent, measurable benefit of the doubt on discretionary decisions — yes. Statistically demonstrable orchestration — no. On primary data the discipline counts are symmetric; the pattern lives entirely in the high-leverage judgment calls, it is directional and borderline-significant, the individual mechanisms are mostly defensible, and the tournament ended with the whistle going against Argentina when it mattered most.

Strict list (the 10 above): P(X ≥ 8 | n=10) = (45+10+1)/1024 ≈ 0.055.

Reading: the signal sits between p ≈ 0.03 and p ≈ 0.15. It crosses the conventional 0.05 line only if lawful-but-one-directional VAR interventions count as "favor," and only if media incident-cataloguing is unbiased — which it is not: controversies involving the tournament's most-watched team are recorded at a higher rate (ascertainment bias inflates the "for" column).

Test 2 — Card leniency (Poisson, tournament rate) — recomputed on primary data

The v1 version of this test is retracted. It was built on the secondary-reported claim of one yellow per 19.7 fouls through the quarterfinals — sourced from the same reporting chain that inverted the Jordan foul counts. Exact primary-feed totals (ESPN boxscores, all cells closed):

Through the QF stage (M1–M6): Argentina 73 fouls, 6 yellows → one per 12.2 fouls. Expected at the 9.5-foul field rate: λ = 73/9.5 ≈ 7.7; observed 6; one-sided Poisson P(X ≤ 6 | λ=7.7) ≈ 0.35 — null. Not close.
Full tournament (M1–M8): Argentina 113 fouls, 13 yellows + 1 red → one booking per 8.7 fouls — marginally stricter than the field average. Their opponents across the same eight matches: 107 fouls, 12 yellows + 1 red → one per 8.9. Symmetric to within rounding.

Conclusion: on primary data there is no card-leniency signal at all. The per-match texture that fed the narrative is real but self-cancelling — Egypt's stoppage-time card string and Argentina's clean sheet in that match (0 on 13 fouls, p ≈ 0.24) sit against Argentina being out-carded 3–1 by England's sheet and 3–1 in extra time against Switzerland, and Spain's 21-foul, zero-card final. Game-state and possession confounds explain the residue without any recourse to bias.

What the tests cannot see

Selection of which incidents become "controversies" is not random; low-weight calls in Argentina's favor were catalogued by a global press corps following Messi, while equivalent calls in, say, Colombia–Uzbekistan were not. Conversely, the counted-stats tests can't price single decisions: the Zico and Embolo calls each shifted more win probability than a season of card asymmetry. Both limitations are permanent features of officiating analysis; the honest posture is to publish the range, not a point estimate.

How to read this report

Methodology

Decision rule. A match is flagged on counted metrics only when an officiating count (cards, penalties, VAR interventions) falls outside the 95% Poisson band versus the tournament baseline. Discretionary decisions are logged per match with a direction and an impact weight — red card or goal-value decision 3, penalty 2, yellow card 1 — and tested in aggregate with a binomial test against a 50/50 null. Reviewed-and-cleared VAR checks and factual (semi-automated offside) decisions are recorded but excluded from the weighted ledger. Every match page carries a "denied to opponent" line covering appeals and claims the opposition did not get.

Baselines. Tournament-wide discipline: 22.8 fouls and 2.4 yellow cards per match (32-match checkpoint, ESPN feed aggregation), 2.52 yellows and 0.141 reds per match (NetSI Sport, Northeastern), 13 red cards by July 6 including two under the new mouth-covering rule. Fouls-per-yellow field rate 22.8 / 2.4 = 9.5.

Provenance. Counted stats come from primary feeds — as of v2, directly from the ESPN boxscore and key-events timeline for all eight matches (event ids 760433, 760456, 760483, 760500, 760509, 760513, 760515, 760517; snapshot July 20, 2026), cross-checked against the FIFA match centre, Opta and 365Scores. Contested-call assessments come from named analysts and formal statements (ESPN's VAR review column, Fox's officiating expert, referee-analysis outlets, federation protests, coach quotes). Where a secondary outlet conflicted with primary feeds — the Jordan foul totals, and now the "19.7 fouls per card" claim — the primary feed wins and the conflict is disclosed in place.

Known limitations. n = 8 matches; ascertainment bias in incident cataloguing; game-state and possession confounds on card rates. This report publishes significance ranges rather than point claims. Officiating assessments quoted here are the professional opinions of the named analysts, not findings of fact.
