Four findings that survive scrutiny — stated plainly

Each card: the claim, the evidence, and what would falsify it. Claims that depend on a particular definition say so.

Finding 1 · counted record

Egypt paid the most.

Hardest-carded team of all 48 (one booking per 4.9 fouls vs the 8.6 field average), zero penalties won and two conceded — and the two heaviest contested calls of the Round of 16 (the Zico disallowed goal, the Fathy penalty appeal) both went against them in a single match they led 2–0 with 11 minutes left.

Falsifiable how? The discipline burden is objective and stands under any definition. The contested-call burden softens under the strict count (both calls were rated defensible by at least one expert) — the chart above shows exactly that.

Finding 2 · counted record

The champion was carded least — and nobody wrote that story.

Spain: one booking per 16.8 fouls across eight matches — nearly twice the field average, including 21 fouls with zero cards in the final itself. Norway (19.3) was the tournament's most leniently carded side overall. The favoritism coverage chased Argentina, whose rate (8.7) matched the field almost exactly.

Falsifiable how? Leniency correlates with possession and leading game-state everywhere in football — see the confound panel below. The numbers are facts; "favoritism" would be an inference the counted record alone cannot carry.

Finding 3 · contested ledger

The Argentina pattern is real — and it still wasn't enough.

Argentina lead the contested-call ledger under every definition (net +11 weighted on the standard count; 8 of 10 flagged calls their way per the Whistle Ledger). But the two heaviest calls split the experts, the card record was field-average, and the final inverted the pattern: a correct red card against them, and they lost to a team whose own goal had been wrongly disallowed.

Falsifiable how? Ascertainment bias — calls involving the most-watched team get catalogued more. If equivalent incidents in low-profile matches went unrecorded, the +11 shrinks. That limitation is permanent and disclosed.

Finding 4 · institutional record

The deepest wounds were off the pitch.

A red-card ban suspended after public pressure from a head of state (UEFA: FIFA "crossed a red line"); a referee-of-the-year barred from entry on unevidenced claims; an all-Argentine crew appointed to a quarterfinal on Argentina's potential final path. No on-field call did as much damage to trust in the process as the process itself.

Falsifiable how? These are documented events, not statistics. The counter-reading — isolated lapses, not a pattern — is genuinely open; the ledger lists them as unresolved, not as verdicts.

The counted record: cards per foul, all 48 teams

One row per team: how many fouls it took to earn a booking (yellow or red). Longer bar = more lenient treatment. Solid fills — this layer is objective.

Read this before concluding anything: card rates confound with style and game state. Possession sides commit fewer desperate fouls; leading teams get carded less everywhere in football — and the deep-run teams here (Spain 8 matches, Norway 6) led for most of their minutes. The one natural experiment cuts the other way: pressed and trailing in the final, Argentina drew five bookings and a red in one night. Leniency numbers are facts; intent is not derivable from them.

Method & sources

Data. Counted stats: ESPN boxscores + timelines for all 104 matches, snapshot 2026-07-20, frozen in this site's archive. Incidents: compiled from Sports Illustrated's ranked 13, The VAR Verdict's ranked 10 (with per-call correct/incorrect verdicts), ESPN's VAR review column, Fox Sports' officiating experts, AP, and Al Jazeera's tournament coverage — every match context cross-verified against the archive (several outlets' round labels were corrected: Balogun R32 #81, Kane R32 #80, Gvardiol R32 #83).

Weights. Goal-value decision or red card = 3 · penalty = 2 · card-threshold = 1 — the Whistle Ledger convention.

Limits. n is small; incident cataloguing skews toward famous teams; "correct" is an expert judgment, not a ruling. This page publishes ranges and toggles rather than a single verdict — deliberately.
